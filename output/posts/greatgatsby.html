The Gatsby President and the Math of Moral Decay

America’s soul is eroding at a calculable rate, and the man in the gold room keeps hosting parties.

By Kevin Day

Some ironies are so grotesque they can’t even be called irony anymore—they’re diagnosis. Donald Trump, hosting a “Great Gatsby”-themed gala while the nation’s poor brace for a government shutdown, isn’t a metaphor. It’s a measurement of moral entropy in real time.

Trump has always been the anti-Gatsby: no yearning, no lost love—just appetite. Gatsby reached for the green light; Trump flicks it on and sells tickets. America, watching, mistakes the glow for glory.

RippleTruth analysis puts our national coherence at FILS = 0.87 (strong moral intent) and TTCF = 0.94 (near-total chaos). That math tells a simple truth: our collective intention is collapsing under the weight of its own noise.

Isociety(t) = I0 · e−λchaost  where λchaos ≈ 0.94

That’s the decay curve of a country drunk on grievance and delusion. Every Mar-a-Lago gala accelerates the half-life of American empathy. Each tweet, each cheap applause line, widens the moral gap between what we once were and what we tolerate now.

In physics, energy lost to friction becomes heat. In intention theory, meaning lost to greed becomes chaos. You can calculate it:

M = 1 / (1 + W × C)

Here, M is moral coherence, W is wealth concentration, and C is chaos. When both rise—as they have under Trump—the denominator grows, and the nation’s moral temperature falls toward zero. No prayer breakfast can reverse that curve until the equation itself is rewritten.

“They smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money...” —F. Scott Fitzgerald

The original Gatsby ended face-down in a swimming pool. America’s version might end in a ballroom, champagne still fizzing, the orchestra still playing while the lights flicker out. Trump isn’t Gatsby’s ghost—he’s the arithmetic of what happens when we celebrate our own collapse.

And somewhere outside those gilded gates, the hungry are still waiting for dinner that will never come—because the president took the tablecloth and turned it into a costume.

Kevin Day writes about politics, culture, and intention theory. He is a U.S. Navy veteran and the author of “The Unified Cosmic Intention Postulate.”
